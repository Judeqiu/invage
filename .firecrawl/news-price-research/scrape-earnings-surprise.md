Definition

An earnings surprise is when a company's reported profits significantly differ from analysts' expectations, either exceeding or falling short of those estimates.

### Key Takeaways

- Earnings surprises occur when a company's profits exceed or fall short of analyst expectations.
- Positive earnings surprises can lead to immediate and sustained stock price increases.
- Analysts use various models and company-provided guidance to estimate earnings per share.
- Management's guidance and market conditions play a significant role in setting earnings expectations.
- A discounted cash flow (DCF) analysis is a common method for evaluating a company's investment potential.

An earnings surprise occurs when a company's reported quarterly or annual profits are above or below analysts' expectations. These [analysts](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/a/analyst.asp), who work for a variety of financial firms and reporting agencies, base their expectations on a variety of sources, including previous quarterly or [annual reports](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/a/annualreport.asp) and current market conditions, as well as the company's own earnings predictions or " [guidance](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/g/guidance.asp)."

## Understanding the Impact of Earnings Surprises

In order to [create an accurate forecast](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/active-trading/022315/stock-analysis-forecasting-revenue-and-growth.asp) of how a specific company’s stock will perform, an analyst must gather information from several sources. They need to speak with the company’s management, visit that company, study its products, and closely watch the industry in which it operates. Then, the analyst will create a mathematical model that incorporates what they have learned and reflects their judgment or expectation of the company’s earnings for the forthcoming quarter. The expectations may be published on the company's website and distributed to the analyst’s clients. A surprise occurs when a company reports numbers that deviate from those estimates.

Earnings surprises can have a huge impact on a company's stock price. Several studies suggest that positive earnings surprises not only lead to an immediate hike in a stock's price, but also to a gradual increase over time.12 Hence, it's not surprising that some companies are known for routinely beating earnings projections. A negative earnings surprise will usually result in a decline in share price.

### Important

Publicly traded companies also issue their own guidance outlining expected future profits or losses. This forecast helps financial analysts set expectations and can be compared to gauge potential company performance in the upcoming quarter.

## How Analysts Estimate Earnings and Handle Surprises

Analysts spend an enormous amount of time before companies report their results, trying to predict [earnings per share](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/e/eps.asp) (EPS) and other metrics. Many analysts use forecasting models, management guidance, and other fundamental information to derive EPS estimates. A [discounted cash flow model](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/dcf.asp), or DCF, is a popular intrinsic valuation method.

DCF analyses use future free cash flow projections and discount them via a required annual rate. The result of the valuation process is a present value estimate. This, in turn, is used to evaluate the company’s potential for investment. If the DCF value exceeds the investment's current cost, the opportunity could be a good one.

Analysts rely on a variety of fundamental factors in the companies’ SEC filings (e.g., SEC Form [10-Q](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/1/10q.asp) for a quarterly report and SEC Form [10-K](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/1/10-k.asp) for its more comprehensive annual report). In both reports, the [management discussion and analysis](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/m/mdanalysis.asp) (MD&A) section provides a detailed overview of the previous period’s operations, how the company performed financially, and how management is planning to move forward in the coming reporting period.

Management's discussion and analysis delves into the specific reasons behind aspects of the company's growth or decline on the income statement, balance sheet, and statement of cash flows. The section breaks down growth drivers, risks, and even pending litigation (often also in the footnotes). Management also frequently uses the MD&A section to announce upcoming goals and approaches to new projects, as well as any changes in the executive suite and/or key hires.

## The Bottom Line

Earnings surprises—when a company’s actual profits differ from expectations—can strongly influence stock prices. Positive surprises tend to lift stock prices, while negative ones usually push them down, making analyst forecasts and company guidance crucial to market reactions.
