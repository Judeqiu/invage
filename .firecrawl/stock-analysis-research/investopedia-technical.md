Definition

Technical analysis is a method used by traders to forecast future price movements by analyzing past market data, primarily focusing on price and volume variations.

### Key Takeaways

- Technical analysis evaluates price trends and volume patterns to identify potential investments and trading opportunities.

- It contrasts with fundamental analysis, as it relies on past price movements rather than financial metrics to predict future trends.

- Analysts use various tools like chart patterns, momentum indicators, and moving averages to forecast price changes.

- Critics argue that technical analysis can be a self-fulfilling prophecy and emphasize that prices may follow a random walk.

## What Is Technical Analysis?

Technical analysis is a popular trading method that evaluates trading activity data, focusing on price and volume, to find investment opportunities and determine optimal trade entry and exit points. It contrasts with fundamental analysis, which evaluates a security's value based on financial data such as earnings and sales.

Technical analysis heavily relies on past market data; however, it's important to understand that past performance does not guarantee future results. Traders choosing to adopt this trading discipline should ensure they have a [top online brokerage platform](https://www.investopedia.com/best-online-brokers-4587872) that's not only cost-effective but also provides them with the necessary trading tools, access to market research, and educational materials to keep up to date with various trading strategies.

## Deep Dive into Technical Analysis

Technical analysis is used to scrutinize the ways supply and demand for a security affect changes in price, volume, and implied volatility. It assumes that past trading activity and price changes of a [security](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/security.asp) can be valuable indicators of the security's future price movements when paired with appropriate investing or trading rules.

Technical analysis's various charting tools are often used to generate short-term trading signals. They also help assess a security's strength or weakness compared to the broader market or specific sectors. Analysts use this information to improve overall valuation estimates.

However, there are several notable [pioneers of the technical analysis](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/financial-theory/10/pioneers-technical-analysis.asp) discipline, including William P. Hamilton, Robert Rhea, Edson Gould, and John Magee, who further contributed to Dow Theory concepts.12 Technical analysis has evolved to include hundreds of patterns and signals developed through years of research.

### Practical Applications of Technical Analysis

Professional analysts often use technical analysis in conjunction with other forms of research. Retail traders often rely on price charts and similar stats to make decisions. But practicing equity analysts rarely limit their research to fundamental or technical analysis alone.

You can apply technical analysis to any security with historical trading data. This includes stocks, [derivatives](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/derivative.asp), [commodities](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/commodity.asp), fixed-income securities, currencies, and more. Technical analysis is common in commodities and forex markets where traders focus on short-term price changes.

Technical analysis attempts to forecast the price movement of virtually any tradable instrument that is generally subject to forces of supply and demand. Some view technical analysis as simply the supply and demand forces reflected by the market price movements of a security.

Technical analysis most commonly applies to price changes, but some analysts track numbers other than just price, such as trading volume or open interest figures.

### Key Technical Indicators for Investors

Researchers have developed hundreds of patterns and signals throughout the years to support technical analysis trading. Technical analysts have also developed numerous types of trading systems to aid them in forecasting and trading on price movements.

Some indicators identify current market trends, including areas of support and resistance. Others assess a trend's strength and whether it will continue. Commonly used technical indicators and charting patterns include trendlines, channels, moving averages, and momentum indicators.

In general, technical analysts look at the following broad types of indicators:

- Price trends
- Chart patterns
- Volume and [momentum](https://www.investopedia.com/investing/momentum-and-relative-strength-index/) indicators
- [Oscillators](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/o/oscillator.asp)
- Moving averages
- Support and resistance levels

The CMT Association supports the largest collection of chartered or certified analysts using technical analysis professionally around the world. The association's Chartered Market Technician (CMT) designation requires passing three levels of exams that cover a broad and in-depth look at technical analysis tools.3

## Core Principles of Technical Analysis

Technical analysis attempts to decipher the market sentiment behind price trends by looking for price patterns and trends. Charles Dow released a series of editorials discussing technical analysis theory. He had two basic assumptions that continue to form the framework for technical analysis trading.4

- Markets are efficient with values that represent factors influencing a security's price.
- Even random market price movements appear to move in identifiable patterns and trends that tend to repeat over time.

Today, the field of technical analysis builds on Dow's work. Professional analysts generally accept three assumptions:

1. **The market discounts everything:** Technical analysts believe that everything from a company's fundamentals to broad market factors to [market psychology](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/m/marketpsychology.asp) is already priced into a stock. The [Efficient Markets Hypothesis (EMH)](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/e/efficientmarkethypothesis.asp) draws a similar conclusion about prices. The only thing remaining is the analysis of price movements, which technical analysts view as the product of supply and demand for a particular stock.
2. **Price moves in trends:** Technical analysts expect prices, even in random market movements, to exhibit trends regardless of the time frame observed. In other words, a stock price is more likely to continue a past trend than to move erratically. Most technical trading strategies are based on this assumption.
3. **History tends to repeat itself:** The repetitive nature of price movements is often attributed to market psychology, which tends to be very predictable, based on human emotions such as fear and excitement. Technical analysis uses chart patterns to analyze these emotions and subsequent price movements to understand trends. While many forms of technical analysis have been used for more than 100 years, they are still believed to be relevant because they illustrate patterns in price movements that often repeat themselves.

## Comparing Fundamental and Technical Analysis

Fundamental analysis and technical analysis, the two major schools of thought when it comes to approaching the markets, are at polar opposite ends of the spectrum. Both methods are used to research and [forecast future trends in stock prices](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/t/technical-analysis-of-stocks-and-trends.asp), and, like any investment strategy or philosophy, each has its pros and cons.

### Fundamental Analysis

Fundamental analysis is a method of evaluating securities by attempting to measure the [intrinsic value](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/i/intrinsicvalue.asp) of a stock. Fundamental analysts study everything from the overall economy and industry conditions to a company's financial condition and management. Reports such as earnings, [expenses](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/e/expense.asp), assets, and liabilities are all important characteristics of fundamental analysis that help analysts determine the fair value of a business.

### Technical Analysis

Technical analysis differs from fundamental analysis in that the stock's price and volume are the only inputs. The main belief is that all known fundamentals are already reflected in the price, so they aren’t closely examined. Technical analysts do not attempt to measure a security's intrinsic value, but instead, [use stock charts to identify patterns](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/forex/09/mcginley-dynamic-indicator.asp) and trends that suggest how a stock's price will move in the future.

## Challenges and Limitations of Technical Analysis

**First challenge and limitation**: For some analysts and academic researchers, the EMH demonstrates why no actionable information is contained in historical price and volume data. However, by the same reasoning, business fundamentals should not provide actionable information. These points of view are known as the weak form and semi-strong form of the EMH.

**Second challenge and limitation**: Another criticism of technical analysis is that history does not repeat itself exactly, so price pattern studies are of dubious importance and can be ignored. Prices seem to be better modeled as random walks.

**Third challenge and limitation**:The last criticism of technical analysis is that it works in some cases, but only because it constitutes a self-fulfilling prophecy. For example, many technical traders will place a [stop-loss order](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/stop-lossorder.asp) below a company's 200-day moving average.

If a large number of traders have done so and the stock reaches this price, there will be many sell orders, pushing the stock price down, confirming the anticipated movement.

Then, other traders will see the price decrease and sell their positions, reinforcing the strength of the trend. This short-term selling pressure can be considered self-fulfilling, but it will have little bearing on where the asset's price will be weeks or months from now.

In sum, if enough people use the same signals, they could cause the movement foretold by the signal. However, this sole group of traders cannot drive the price over the long run.

## What Assumptions Do Technical Analysts Make?

Professional technical analysts typically assume three things. First, the market discounts everything. Second, prices, even in random market movements, will exhibit trends regardless of the time frame being observed. Third, history tends to repeat itself. The repetitive nature of price movements is often attributed to market psychology, which tends to be very predictable.

## What's the Difference Between Fundamental and Technical Analysis?

Fundamental analysis is a method of evaluating securities by attempting to measure the intrinsic value of a stock. The core assumption of technical analysis, on the other hand, is that all known fundamentals are factored into price; thus, there is no need to pay close attention to them. Technical analysts do not attempt to measure a security's intrinsic value, but instead, use stock charts to identify patterns and trends that might suggest how the security's price will move in the future.

## How Can I Learn Technical Analysis?

Your first step is to learn about investing, stocks, markets, and financials. This can be done through books, [online courses](https://www.investopedia.com/the-best-technical-analysis-courses-11705192) and materials, and in-person classes. Once you understand the basics, you can start [studying technical analysis](https://www.investopedia.com/trading/best-ways-learn-technical-analysis/).

## The Bottom Line

Technical analysis remains a vital tool for evaluating securities' price movements by analyzing historical trading data. Unlike fundamental analysis, it focuses on past trading behavior, price trends, and volume to predict future movements. This approach uses various indicators such as trendlines, moving averages, and momentum indicators to help traders identify entry and exit points.

Understanding the assumptions underlying technical analysis, including market efficiency, price trends, and repeating history, empowers investors to navigate the complexities of financial markets effectively.
