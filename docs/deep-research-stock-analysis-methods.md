# Deep Research: Ways to Analyze and Evaluate Stocks

**Date:** 2026-07-12  
**Depth:** Thorough (~15–25 sources)  
**Scope:** Major schools of stock analysis and evaluation — absolute and relative valuation, financial-statement analysis, value/growth/quality (moat) frameworks, technical analysis, factor/quantitative models, behavioral pitfalls, and market-efficiency critiques.

---

## Executive Summary

Stock analysis is not a single method. Practitioners and academics use overlapping but distinct toolkits that answer different questions: *What is this business worth?* (intrinsic valuation), *Is it cheap relative to peers?* (multiples), *Is the market mispricing it?* (value/growth), *How strong is the franchise?* (quality/moat), *When might price move?* (technicals), and *What systematic risks explain returns?* (factor models).

Across high-quality sources, several patterns are consistent:

1. **Valuation splits into absolute vs. relative models.** Absolute models (DDM, DCF, residual income, asset-based) try to estimate intrinsic value from fundamentals alone. Relative models (P/E, P/B, P/S, EV/EBITDA, etc.) benchmark a stock against peers or history and are faster but do not produce a “true” value. Neither class is universally superior; the right tool depends on cash-flow predictability, dividends, industry norms, and data quality.

2. **Industry context dominates practice.** Sell-side and research analysts do not apply one model everywhere: banking leans on P/B, insurance on embedded value, consumer goods on P/CF, REITs on P/AFFO, while pharma/energy/telecom more often use DCF. Theoretical models such as residual income (RIM) and abnormal earnings growth (AEG) see limited real-world use.

3. **DCF is powerful but fragile.** Two-stage DCF (near-term free cash flows plus terminal value) is standard for mature firms with stable positive FCF. CFA practitioners warn that terminal value can account for ~80% of a DCF and rests on growth and discount-rate assumptions that break under disruption (Kodak, BlackBerry). Treat DCF as a *range and discipline*, not a single precise target.

4. **Investment styles are analytical filters, not mutually exclusive tools.** Value (Graham/Buffett margin of safety), growth and GARP (P/E, PEG, FCF), and moat/quality (sustainable competitive advantage at a fair price) share financial-statement roots but prioritize different metrics and holding horizons.

5. **Technicals address timing and psychology, not business value.** Charts, support/resistance, RSI, MACD, and patterns help with entries, exits, and shorter-term risk management. Major brokers and educators stress that technical analysis is **not** a sole research method for long-term investment decisions.

6. **Factor models reframe “alpha.”** Fama–French three- and five-factor models show that much of diversified-portfolio return variation is explained by market, size, value, profitability, and investment (plus optional momentum/quality/low-vol). Apparent stock-picking skill often collapses into known factor exposures.

7. **Markets are *approximately* efficient for most investors.** Fama, Thaler, and Malkiel converge on a practical implication: after fees, most active managers fail to beat passive benchmarks consistently. Behavioral biases (overconfidence, herding, loss aversion, confirmation bias, anchoring) explain why individuals underperform their own analysis. Analysis still matters for risk management, process, and valuation ranges — not for guaranteed outperformance.

**Bottom line:** Robust stock evaluation is multi-method. Use financial statements and industry-appropriate valuation as the core; add style filters (value/growth/moat); use factors for portfolio construction and performance attribution; use technicals sparingly for timing; and design process against behavioral failure modes.

## Key Findings

1. **Absolute vs. relative valuation is the primary taxonomy.** Absolute models estimate intrinsic value from dividends, cash flows, residual income, or assets. Relative models compare multiples (P/E, P/B, P/S, P/CF, EV multiples) to peers under the Law of One Price. Relative methods are quicker; absolute methods are deeper but assumption-heavy.  
   Source: [Investopedia — Best Stock Valuation Methods](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/fundamental-analysis/11/choosing-valuation-methods.asp)

2. **DDM fits stable dividend payers; DCF fits non-payers with predictable FCF; comps are the fallback.** Gordon Growth (constant-growth DDM) needs consistent payout and growth. Two-stage DCF needs positive, forecastable free cash flow (often mature firms). Comparables work when earnings/cash metrics are meaningful and peers are truly comparable.  
   Source: [Investopedia — Valuation Methods](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/fundamental-analysis/11/choosing-valuation-methods.asp); [Investopedia — DCF](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/dcf.asp); [CFI — DCF Formula Guide](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/valuation/dcf-formula-guide/)

3. **Terminal value is the main DCF risk.** CFA analysis notes terminal value can drive ~80% of model output and depends on perpetual growth and WACC-like discount assumptions. Holding periods have shortened dramatically (historical multi-year ownership vs. months today), which weakens the case for relying on distant cash flows alone.  
   Source: [CFA Institute — DCF Dilemma](https://rpc.cfainstitute.org/blogs/enterprising-investor/2025/the-discounted-cash-flow-dilemma-a-tool-for-theorists-or-practitioners)

4. **Industry-specific models dominate professional practice.** Literature review of analyst behavior: banks → P/B; insurance → EV; consumer goods → P/CF; REITs → P/AFFO; selected sectors (pharma, energy, telecom) → DCF; RIM/AEG rarely used outside niches. Post-2000, DCF usage rose but still often trails P/E.  
   Source: [Olbert — Industry-specific stock valuation methods (literature review)](https://www.diva-portal.org/smash/get/diva2:1857938/FULLTEXT01.pdf)

5. **Financial statements remain the raw material of fundamental analysis.** Income statement, balance sheet, and cash-flow statement (plus 10-K narrative sections) are the SEC/FINRA/HBS starting point for liquidity, solvency, profitability, and cash generation. The 10-K is the primary annual document for U.S. public companies.  
   Sources: [SEC — Beginners’ Guide to Financial Statements](https://www.sec.gov/about/reports-publications/investorpubsbegfinstmtguide); [Investor.gov — How to Read a 10-K](https://www.investor.gov/introduction-investing/getting-started/researching-investments/how-read-10-k); [FINRA](https://www.finra.org/investors/insights/financial-statements-investment-opportunities); [HBS Online](https://online.hbs.edu/blog/post/how-to-read-financial-statements)

6. **Value investing is intrinsic-value-minus-price with a margin of safety.** Graham/Dodd lineage: market overreacts; buy below estimated intrinsic value; require a margin of safety (Graham classically emphasized large discounts to liquidation/intrinsic estimates). Metrics include financial performance, cash flow, competitive position; process is long-horizon and often contrarian.  
   Source: [Investopedia — Value Investing](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/v/valueinvesting.asp)

7. **Growth evaluation centers on P/E, PEG, sales multiples, and FCF quality.** PEG = P/E ÷ expected earnings growth; PEG ~1 often cited as “fair” relative to growth (with caveats). High growth can justify high P/E only if growth is durable and cash-flow backed.  
   Sources: [Investopedia — P/E and PEG](https://www.investopedia.com/investing/use-pe-ratio-and-peg-to-tell-stocks-future/); [Interactive Brokers Campus — Ratios for Growth Stocks](https://www.interactivebrokers.com/campus/trading-lessons/using-ratios-to-identify-growth-stocks/)

8. **Moat/quality analysis asks whether advantages are durable.** “Moat investing” targets sustainable competitive advantages (e.g., intangible assets, cost advantages, switching costs, network effects, efficient scale — as popularized in Morningstar-style frameworks) **and** attractive valuation, not quality alone.  
   Source: [VanEck — Moat Investing](https://www.vaneck.com/us/en/insights/moat-investing/)

9. **Technical analysis is price/volume-based and complementary.** Candlesticks (OHLC), trends (HH/HL vs. LH/LL), support/resistance, RSI (overbought/oversold), MACD (momentum), and chart patterns. Useful for timing; Schwab and Babson materials position it as short-/medium-term and best combined with fundamentals.  
   Sources: [Investopedia — Technical Analysis](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/t/technicalanalysis.asp); [Charles Schwab — Reading Charts](https://www.schwab.com/learn/story/how-to-read-stock-charts-and-trading-patterns); [Babson Cutler Center — Technical Analysis Intro (PDF)](https://www.babson.edu/media/babson/assets/cutler-center/Introduciton-to-Technical-Analysis.pdf)

10. **Factor models explain most diversified returns.** Three-factor: market excess return, SMB (size), HML (value). Five-factor adds RMW (profitability) and CMA (investment/conservative vs. aggressive). Claimed explanatory power for diversified portfolios is high (~95% in classic FF summaries). Momentum and quality are widely used extensions.  
    Sources: [Investopedia — Fama–French](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/f/famaandfrenchthreefactormodel.asp); [Robeco — Five-factor critique](https://www.robeco.com/en-int/insights/2024/10/fama-french-5-factor-model-why-more-is-not-always-better); [Alpha Architect — Factor insights](https://alphaarchitect.com/factor-investing-insights-you-wont-hear-from-fama-and-french/)

11. **EMH and behavioral finance are two sides of the same coin.** EMH: prices reflect available information (weak/semi-strong/strong forms). Fama and Thaler largely agree that *beating the market after costs is hard*; they disagree more on whether prices are always “correct.” Malkiel: professional managers systematically underperform indexes; anomalies rarely yield reliable excess returns after costs.  
    Sources: [Chicago Booth Review — Are Markets Efficient?](https://www.chicagobooth.edu/review/are-markets-efficient); [Malkiel — The Efficient Market Hypothesis and Its Critics (PDF)](https://www.princeton.edu/~ceps/workingpapers/91malkiel.pdf)

12. **Behavioral biases systematically degrade analysis quality.** Overconfidence, herding, loss aversion/disposition effect, confirmation bias, anchoring, and recency bias lead investors to ignore process, overtrade, and misweight evidence.  
    Sources: [Investopedia — Behavioral Finance](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/b/behavioralfinance.asp); [William & Mary — Behavioral Biases](https://online.mason.wm.edu/blog/behavioral-biases-that-can-impact-investing-decisions)

## Detailed Analysis

### 1. Taxonomy: What “stock analysis” means

| Family | Core question | Typical tools | Horizon |
|--------|---------------|---------------|---------|
| **Fundamental / financial-statement** | Is the business healthy and cash-generative? | 10-K, IS/BS/CF, ratios | Medium–long |
| **Absolute valuation** | What is intrinsic value? | DDM, DCF, RIM, asset-based | Medium–long |
| **Relative valuation** | Cheap vs. peers/history? | P/E, PEG, P/B, P/S, EV/EBITDA | Short–long |
| **Style: value** | Discount to intrinsic with safety margin? | MOS, liquidation, quality at discount | Long |
| **Style: growth / GARP** | Growth durable and not overpriced? | PEG, sales growth, FCF margin | Medium–long |
| **Style: quality / moat** | Advantage sustainable at fair price? | Moat sources + valuation screen | Long |
| **Technical** | Where is price/volume momentum? | Charts, RSI, MACD, S/R | Short–medium |
| **Quantitative / factor** | What systematic exposures drive returns? | FF3/FF5, momentum, quality, low-vol | Portfolio / multi-year |
| **Behavioral / process** | Am I fooling myself? | Checklists, pre-mortems, rules | Continuous |

These families compose; professionals rarely use only one.

---

### 2. Financial-statement analysis (the foundation)

Before any valuation model, analysis rests on audited (or review-level) financials and disclosures:

- **Income statement:** revenue, costs, margins, earnings quality  
- **Balance sheet:** assets, liabilities, equity, leverage, liquidity  
- **Cash-flow statement:** operating cash flow vs. earnings, capex, free cash flow, financing  
- **10-K narrative:** business description, risk factors, MD&A, legal proceedings  

SEC investor materials emphasize that the three statements must be read *together*. FINRA and HBS guides stress ratios for liquidity (e.g., current ratio), solvency (debt metrics), and profitability (margins, ROE/ROIC). The 10-K is the canonical annual package for U.S. issuers; Investor.gov documents how to navigate it.

**Practical evaluation stack:**

1. Earnings quality: cash from operations vs. net income; accruals  
2. Balance-sheet risk: net debt, covenants, off-balance items, goodwill  
3. Capital allocation: reinvestment, buybacks, dividends, M&A  
4. Trend and peer context: multi-year margins, ROIC, working-capital cycles  

**Limitation:** Statements are historical and accounting-policy dependent; they do not equal economic value without a valuation bridge.

### 3. Absolute valuation models

#### 3.1 Dividend Discount Model (DDM)

**Idea:** Equity value = present value of expected dividends.  
**Best fit:** Stable, mature payers with consistent payout ratio and growth (Gordon Growth Model when growth is constant).  
**Weak fit:** Non-payers, irregular dividends, high-growth firms reinvesting all earnings.

#### 3.2 Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)

**Idea:** Value = discounted expected free cash flows (to firm or equity) + terminal value.  
**Common structure:** Explicit forecast 5–10 years → terminal value (Gordon growth or exit multiple) → discount at WACC (enterprise) or cost of equity (equity).  
**Best fit:** Positive, reasonably predictable FCF; typically mature businesses.  
**Weak fit:** Pre-profit startups, highly cyclical firms without normalized mid-cycle FCF, rapid disruption.

**CFA practitioner critique (2025):**

- DCF creates an **illusion of precision** despite fragile inputs.  
- **Terminal value** can dominate (~80%) the result.  
- Corporate mortality and industry disruption (Kodak, BlackBerry) invalidate perpetual-growth optimism.  
- Shorter investor holding periods weaken reliance on far-horizon cash.  
- Better use: force disciplined thinking about economics; buy at a **discount to a range** of scenarios, not a single spreadsheet point estimate.

#### 3.3 Other absolute approaches

- **Residual income / excess earnings models:** book value + PV of residual income (theoretically elegant; little analyst adoption outside niches).  
- **Asset-based / liquidation / sum-of-the-parts:** useful for asset-heavy, distressed, or conglomerate cases.

### 4. Relative valuation (multiples)

Multiples implement the **Law of One Price**: similar assets should trade at similar prices.

| Multiple | Numerator | Denominator | Typical use |
|----------|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| P/E | Price | Earnings | Profitable comps; earnings quality critical |
| Forward P/E | Price | Next-year EPS | Growth-sensitive |
| PEG | P/E | EPS growth % | Growth at reasonable price screen |
| P/B | Price | Book equity | Banks, asset-heavy firms |
| P/S | Price | Sales | Early-stage or cyclical with no earnings |
| P/CF or P/FCF | Price | Cash flow | Earnings-distorted cases |
| EV/EBITDA | Enterprise value | EBITDA | Cross-capital-structure comps |
| Industry-specific | Varies | AFFO, embedded value, etc. | REITs, insurers, etc. |

**Rules of good practice:**

- Use **positive, high-quality** earnings/cash metrics.  
- Match **peers carefully** (business mix, growth, capital structure, geography).  
- Prefer **EV multiples** when leverage differs.  
- Cross-check with absolute valuation when stakes are high.

**Limitation:** A sector can be **collectively mispriced**; cheap vs. peers ≠ cheap vs. intrinsic value.

### 5. Style frameworks: value, growth, quality

#### 5.1 Value investing

- **Premise:** Markets overreact; price can diverge from long-term fundamentals.  
- **Process:** Estimate intrinsic value (quantitative + qualitative: brand, model, moat); buy only with **margin of safety**.  
- **Graham emphasis:** deep discounts (e.g., historically two-thirds of conservative value estimates) to limit permanent capital loss.  
- **Buffett/Munger evolution:** quality businesses at fair prices, not only cigar-butt cheapness.  
- **Risks:** value traps (cheap for structural reasons), multi-year underperformance, subjectivity in intrinsic value.

#### 5.2 Growth and GARP

- **Premise:** High reinvestment and earnings growth can drive price appreciation even at elevated multiples.  
- **Tools:** historical and expected EPS growth, P/E, **PEG**, P/S, FCF margins, reinvestment needs.  
- **PEG intuition:** high P/E may be justified if growth is proportionally higher; PEG < 1 often marketed as attractive (context-dependent).  
- **Risks:** growth deceleration, multiple compression, accounting-heavy “earnings” without cash, competition eroding unit economics.

#### 5.3 Moat / quality investing

- **Premise:** Durable competitive advantages support higher ROIC and more reliable long-term cash flows.  
- **Practice (e.g., Morningstar/VanEck framing):** identify wide/narrow moats **and** demand valuation discipline — quality at any price is not the strategy.  
- **Risks:** moats erode; “quality” crowding; paying too much for safety.

### 6. Technical analysis

**Definition:** Use historical price and volume to infer probable future price behavior and market psychology — **not** to value the underlying business.

**Core toolkit:**

- Chart types: line, bar, candlestick (O/H/L/C)  
- Structure: uptrends (higher highs/lows), downtrends, ranges  
- Levels: support and resistance (role reversal after breaks)  
- Indicators: moving averages, RSI (often 70/30 zones), MACD  
- Patterns: head-and-shoulders, double tops/bottoms, candlestick signals (hammer, etc.)  
- Process tip (Babson): seek **multi-indicator confirmation** rather than single-signal trades  

**Role relative to fundamentals:**

- Fundamentals: *what* to own and *why*  
- Technicals: *when* to enter/exit and *how* to manage risk  

Schwab educational content is explicit that technical analysis should not be the **sole** means of investment research.

**Limitations:**

- Patterns can fail; self-fulfilling only when widely followed  
- Overfitting and data mining  
- Poor standalone fit for long-horizon fundamental investing  
- Does not incorporate balance-sheet insolvency or fraud risk

### 7. Quantitative and factor-based evaluation

Rather than valuing one firm in isolation, factor investing asks which **characteristics** systematically relate to average returns.

#### 7.1 Fama–French three-factor model

\[
R_{it} - R_{ft} = \alpha + \beta_1(R_{Mt}-R_{ft}) + \beta_2 SMB_t + \beta_3 HML_t + \epsilon
\]

- **Market:** equity risk premium  
- **SMB (Small Minus Big):** size premium  
- **HML (High Minus Low):** value (high book-to-market) premium  

Interpretation debate: risk-based (efficiency) vs. mispricing-based (behavioral/inefficiency). Classic summaries claim size + value + market explain a large share of diversified portfolio returns.

#### 7.2 Five-factor extension (2014)

Adds:

- **Profitability (RMW):** robust vs. weak operating profitability  
- **Investment (CMA):** conservative vs. aggressive investment/growth in assets  

**Caveats (Robeco and broader literature):** more factors are not always better; factor definitions, construction (annual vs. monthly sorts), and sample periods matter; some “new” factors may be sensitive to methodology. Competing models (q-factor, momentum-augmented models) remain active research.

#### 7.3 Practitioner factor set (beyond FF)

Common implementation factors: **value, momentum, quality, low volatility, size**. Multi-factor portfolios and smart-beta products operationalize these screens at scale.

**Implication for stock evaluation:**

- A “great stock pick” may simply be a high-value, high-quality, positive-momentum bet.  
- Attribute performance to factors before claiming skill.  
- Single-stock factor scores are noisy; factors are portfolio phenomena.

### 8. Industry-specific professional practice

From Olbert’s systematic literature review of analysts’ industry models:

| Sector / setting | Preferred models / factors (reported) |
|------------------|----------------------------------------|
| Banking | Price-to-book (P/B) dominant |
| Insurance | Embedded value approaches |
| Consumer goods | Price-to-cash-flow (P/CF) |
| REITs | Price-to-AFFO |
| Pharma, energy, telecom | More DCF usage |
| Cross-industry | Multiples still pervasive; RIM/AEG rare |

**Takeaway:** Matching the **industry value driver** (book capital for banks, AFFO for REITs, long-cycle cash for energy, pipeline for pharma) matters more than forcing a universal DCF.

### 9. Market efficiency, performance reality, and the role of analysis

**Efficient Market Hypothesis (Fama):** prices reflect available information. Forms: weak (past prices), semi-strong (public info), strong (all info including private).

**Fama–Thaler dialogue (Chicago Booth):**

- Both: hard for investors to **beat the market after costs** (supported by mutual-fund evidence since Jensen).  
- Thaler: “prices are correct” is a separate, harder claim; crashes and excess volatility challenge correctness.  
- Fama: EMH is a useful **approximation**, not a literal law.

**Malkiel’s critique-of-critics:**

- Professional managers, on average, underperform passive indexes by roughly fees.  
- Performance does not persist reliably.  
- Many “anomalies” fail after costs, short-sale constraints, or once publicized.  
- Markets cannot be *perfectly* efficient (Grossman–Stiglitz) or no one would gather information — but they are efficient enough that **easy alpha is rare**.

**What analysis is still for:**

- Risk management and position sizing  
- Avoiding permanent capital loss (fraud, insolvency, obvious overpayment)  
- Building a repeatable process and valuation *ranges*  
- Tax, liquidity, and concentration decisions  
- Understanding factor and sector exposures  

Analysis is **not** a guarantee of outperformance.

### 10. Behavioral finance: evaluating the evaluator

Even excellent models fail if the decision-maker is compromised.

| Bias | How it corrupts stock evaluation |
|------|----------------------------------|
| **Overconfidence** | Too-narrow valuation ranges; excessive trading |
| **Confirmation bias** | Seeking bullish notes only; ignoring risk factors in 10-K |
| **Herding** | Buying narratives at peak multiples because “everyone agrees” |
| **Loss aversion / disposition** | Selling winners too early; holding losers too long |
| **Anchoring** | Fixating on purchase price or a single analyst target |
| **Recency** | Extrapolating last quarter’s growth forever |

**Process antidotes:** written investment theses with invalidation criteria; pre-mortems; checklists; separating analysis from trading; cooling-off periods; tracking decisions vs. outcomes.

---

### 11. Integrating methods into a coherent evaluation workflow

A practical multi-method stack used by serious fundamental investors:

1. **Business & industry map** — model, competition, cycle, regulation  
2. **Financial-statement deep read** — 10-K, cash quality, leverage, capital allocation  
3. **Industry-appropriate valuation** — multiples grid + DCF/DDM *scenario range* if data quality allows  
4. **Style filter** — value (MOS), growth/GARP (PEG + durability), moat/quality  
5. **Portfolio/factor lens** — what systematic bets am I taking?  
6. **Risk & catalyst calendar** — earnings, refinancing, litigation, macro  
7. **Optional technical overlay** — entry/exit, stop discipline (not thesis)  
8. **Behavioral review** — am I anchored, herding, or overconfident?  
9. **Decision** — size, buy/hold/sell/watch, with explicit invalidators  

For **short-term trading**, invert weights: technicals and risk management first; fundamentals as filter.  
For **long-term ownership**, invert again: fundamentals and valuation first; technicals last.

| Technical | Timing, risk rules | No business insight | Entries/exits, trading |
| Factors | Portfolio science | Crowding, definition risk | Construction & attribution |
| Behavioral process | Prevents self-sabotage | Hard to enforce | Always |

## Contrarian Views and Risks

1. **“Just index — analysis is waste.”** Supported by active-manager underperformance data (Malkiel, Jensen lineage; Fama–Thaler agreement on hard-to-beat markets). Counter: concentrated capital, private knowledge domains, risk control, and tax-aware decisions still require judgment. Analysis can be valuable without claiming market-beating alpha.

2. **“DCF is the only rigorous method.”** Contested by CFA “DCF dilemma”: precision is often fake; multiples dominate practice for good reasons (speed, market anchoring). Counter: DCF still forces economic thinking when used as scenarios.

3. **“Technical analysis is astrology.”** Strong academic skepticism of standalone TA profitability after costs. Counter: risk management, liquidity timing, and consensus level awareness have operational value even if “alpha” is dubious.

4. **“Factors killed stock picking.”** Factor zoo risk: data mining, publication bias, and post-discovery decay. Implementation costs, capacity, and regime shifts (value winters) hurt. Factors *explain* more than they *guarantee*.

5. **“Value always wins long term.”** Historical premium is real in many samples but can underperform for long stretches; structural economy shifts (intangibles, zero-rate eras) change book-value meaning.

6. **“Markets are efficient, so prices are always right.”** Thaler and behavioral literature: efficiency as “hard to beat” ≠ “always correct fair value.” Bubbles and crashes are uncomfortable for strict correctness.

7. **Industry-model lock-in risk.** Using P/B for banks is standard — until accounting regimes or business mix (fee vs. net interest) change. Industry rules of thumb lag structural change.

8. **Analyst-target dependence.** Market participants react to analyst revisions, but targets embed the same model errors and incentive biases as the rest of the street. Consensus is not truth.

## Open Questions

1. **How should valuation adapt to intangible-heavy firms** where book equity and traditional capex understate economic investment (software, brands, R&D)?  
2. **What is the half-life of published anomalies and factors** after crowding and AI-driven systematic trading?  
3. **Can hybrid human + ML systems** improve *decision quality* without merely rediscovering known factors?  
4. **How to integrate ESG/climate and geopolitical risk** into DCF discount rates and scenario trees without double-counting narrative?  
5. **Which industry-specific models** retain predictive power for target-price accuracy vs. which are convention only? (Olbert notes this remains an open empirical program.)  
6. **Optimal process design for retail vs. professional contexts** — same tools, different capacity, fees, and behavioral constraints.  
7. **Terminal-value alternatives** (scenario forests, competitive-fade models, reverse DCF from market price) — which best prevent overconfidence?
